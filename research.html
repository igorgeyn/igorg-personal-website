"Electing America's Election Officials" 2024
with Joshua Ferrer · Chapter in The Frontline of Democracy

Data visualization, survey analysis, survey weighting, data collection and integration

We examine the current state and historical details of election administration in the U.S.—which is to say local election administration. We characterize states (and, where relevant, municipalities) in terms of various administration characteristics: who holds final authority, how many individuals oversee elections, nuances of shared-power jurisdictions, and more. We also describe personal characteristics of local election officials, finding that they are on average older and more likely to identify as white.

Working Papers

Do Political Institutions Influence Voters' Policy Knowledge?

Draft available upon request.

Abstract

Do ballot measures enhance civic engagement by increasing voters' knowledge and awareness of policy-relevant information? Using comprehensive data on 1,547 ballot measures across all U.S. states from 2006–2020 merged with 480,383 individual survey responses, I demonstrate how methodological choices fundamentally determine conclusions about this question. Initial analysis using the full set of ballot measures and traditional two-way fixed effects (TWFE) regression suggests that exposure increases news interest by 0.025 points (SE = 0.010, p < 0.05), with effects scaling by intensity and stronger impacts for fiscal measures. Placebo tests strongly support these findings, with the actual effect at the 99th percentile of the null distribution. However, this conclusion fails to hold when accounting for the fact that treatment staggered across states and time results in TWFE making problematic comparisons that can severely bias estimates. Second, defining treatment as the occurrence/incidence of any ballot measure leaves only one state as a never-treated control, preventing use of modern estimators designed for staggered adoption. When I focus on theoretically-motivated morality politics measures (marijuana, gambling, abortion, marriage)—gaining 19 never-treated states and enabling cleaner identification—modern DiD estimators find essentially null effects of -0.0001 and -0.0014 using two commonly used specifications. I validate these results using alternative survey-based measures of voter policy knowledge.

UCLA Voter Guide Project

During Fall 2024, I led a team of UCLA undergraduates in a project that leveraged open-source participation to deliver concise, high-quality, and informative summaries of California ballot initiatives. It was a deeply rewarding project, and I'm grateful to all who participated. The project, which will be kept online as a resource, can be found at sites.google.com/view/ucla-vgp.

A revised and expanded, California-wide update is in the works.
