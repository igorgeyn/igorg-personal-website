Peer-Reviewed Publications

"How Partisan is Local Election Administration?" 2024
with Joshua Ferrer and Daniel M. Thompson · American Political Science Review

The question: Do partisan election officials tilt outcomes in favor of their party? Using a regression discontinuity design around close elections for county clerk, we find they don't—providing evidence that U.S. election administration is more robust to partisan influence than critics suggest.

Methods: regression discontinuity, machine learning, large-scale data integration

Press coverage
  • Science of Politics Podcast
  • Niskanen Center
  • Good Authority
"Do Tuition Subsidies Raise Political Participation?" 2025
with Daniel Firoozi · American Economic Journal: Economic Policy

The question: Does receiving financial aid affect whether students vote? Using California's Cal Grant program and a regression discontinuity at the GPA cutoff, we estimate the causal effect of tuition subsidies on voter registration and turnout—with implications for how states design student aid.

Methods: regression discontinuity, administrative data linkage, econometrics

Findings presented to the California Student Aid Commission. Awarded a California Policy Lab research grant (2022).

"Electing America's Election Officials" 2024
with Joshua Ferrer · Chapter in The Frontline of Democracy

The question: Who actually runs U.S. elections? We provide the first comprehensive descriptive analysis of local election officials—their authority structures, demographics, and how they vary across states. Key finding: officials are older and less diverse than the populations they serve.

Methods: survey analysis, data integration, visualization

Working Papers

Do Political Institutions Influence Voters' Policy Knowledge?

The question: Do ballot measures make voters more informed? Using 1,547 ballot measures across all U.S. states merged with 480,000+ survey responses, I show that the answer depends heavily on methodology—a cautionary tale about difference-in-differences with staggered treatment.

Methods: difference-in-differences, staggered adoption estimators, survey data
