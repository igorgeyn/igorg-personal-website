Your A/B Test Was Representative. Your Analysis Wasn't.

February 20, 2026

TL;DR — Randomized assignment doesn't guarantee a representative analysis sample. Telemetry loss, privacy filters, app-version gaps, and metric-firing conditions all create measurement coverage skew that biases your ATE—even when the experiment itself is perfectly designed. This post walks through a simulated example, compares naive ITT vs. reweighted ITT vs. MRP-style partial-pooling estimators, and gives you a Monday-morning checklist for catching and fixing this in your own experiments.

You did the "right" thing: broad rollout, randomized assignment, no weird eligibility filters. The experiment platform did its job.

Then you sanity-checked the analysis sample against known company demographics…and it was skewed. Not by a little.

  • Web is under-measured.
  • Some regions are missing more than others.
  • New users drop out of logging at higher rates.
  • And your metric is computed only when certain events fire (or fire reliably).

If this sounds familiar: you don't have a randomization problem—you have a measurement coverage problem. And that's an external validity problem even when the experiment is perfectly randomized.

This post is a practical, engineering-style guide to:

  1. diagnosing "representative assignment, skewed measurement,"
  2. deciding whether you should do anything beyond a naive ITT,
  3. and quantifying whether a more sophisticated adjustment (MRP-style) is worth it.

Why this happens in real systems

In a textbook A/B test, every assigned user contributes an outcome. In production, that's rarely true. Here are the most common culprits:

  • Telemetry loss. Client-side logging fails more on low-end devices, spotty networks, and older app versions. Web platforms often lose events to ad blockers and aggressive browser privacy settings. The result: your analysis sample under-represents exactly the users who differ most from your power users.
  • Privacy and consent filters. GDPR opt-outs, CCPA deletions, and app tracking transparency prompts remove users from your analysis non-randomly. Users who opt out tend to be more privacy-conscious, more technically savvy, and often in specific geos (EU, California).
  • Eligibility vs. measurement. A user can be assigned to a treatment arm but never trigger the feature surface (e.g., they don't open the relevant tab, or the feature is gated behind a secondary condition). If your metric only fires when the surface loads, those users vanish from the analysis.
  • Metric definition scope. Many metrics are defined as "among users who did X, what happened to Y?" This conditions on a post-treatment event, which can create collider bias even if the conditioning event seems innocuous.

All of these produce the same signature: the assigned population and the measured population have different covariate distributions, and if treatment effects vary across those covariates, your naive estimate is biased for the population-level ATE.

The setting (realistic, and common in tech)

  • Population: all eligible users assigned to the experiment
  • Analysis dataset: users for whom the metric is actually observed

Those sets differ because "observed" depends on device/network/privacy/app version/edge-case logging paths…not because randomization failed.

The "juice worth the squeeze" harness

We compare three estimators:

  • A — Naive ITT: diff-in-means among users with observed outcome
  • B — Reweighted ITT: poststratification/calibration weights to match the assigned population mix
  • C — MRP-like: partial pool cell-level treatment effects, then poststratify to the assigned mix

A few takeaways:

  1. Naive ITT can be biased if measurement coverage is correlated with covariates and/or outcome.
  2. Reweighting often fixes the headline when skew is explainable by observed covariates.
  3. MRP-like adds a bias–variance trade: it can stabilize thin slices and reduce wiggle, but it won't automatically move you "toward truth" unless the pooling assumptions line up.

Stability under resampling

What to do Monday morning

A checklist for your next experiment review:

  1. Plot assigned vs. measured distributions. Break out every covariate you have (geo, device, platform, tenure, plan tier). If the bars look the same, you're probably fine—stop here and ship the naive ITT.
  2. If there's visible skew, compute reweighted ITT. Poststratification weights = (share in assigned) / (share in measured) for each cell. This is fast and requires nothing beyond crosstabs.
  3. Check weight diagnostics. Effective sample size (ESS), top-1% weight share, and max/median weight ratio. If ESS drops below ~50% of n, or the top 1% of weights accounts for >5% of the total, your cells are too thin and weights are unstable.
  4. If weights are spiky, try partial pooling. Fit a multilevel model (or simple ridge/shrinkage) on cell-level treatment effects, then poststratify. This is the MRP-like approach—it trades a small amount of bias for substantial variance reduction in thin cells.
  5. Report both estimates. If naive and reweighted agree, great—that's a robustness win. If they disagree meaningfully, the difference itself is informative: it tells you the treatment effect varies across the coverage gap.

Diagnostics from this simulation's reweighting step:

  • ESS ≈ 160,609 (out of n = 169,494)
  • Top 1% weight share ≈ 2.23%
  • Max/median weight ratio ≈ 3.14

All healthy—which is why reweighting worked well here and MRP didn't add much.

Gotchas

A few things that can make this harder than the simulation above suggests:

  • MNAR (missing not at random). If missingness depends on the outcome itself—e.g., users who would have churned are also less likely to fire the metric event—then reweighting on observed covariates won't fully fix the bias. You'll need bounds, sensitivity analysis, or a direct fix to the logging pipeline. The simulation above includes mild outcome-dependent missingness; real systems can be worse.
  • Conditioning on post-treatment subsets (collider bias). If your metric is defined as "revenue among users who opened the app at least once this week," you're conditioning on a post-treatment variable (app opens). If treatment affects app opens, you've opened a collider path and your estimate is biased even with perfect coverage. Fix: define metrics on the full assigned population whenever possible (e.g., revenue per assigned user, including zeros).
  • Too many cells, not enough data. If you poststratify on geo × device × tenure × plan × OS version, you'll end up with thousands of cells, many with single-digit counts. Reweighting becomes unstable and MRP becomes essential—but also harder to validate. Start coarse, add dimensions only when the data support it.
  • Multiple metrics, different coverage. Your primary metric and your guardrail metrics may have different measurement coverage patterns. A treatment that looks neutral on a well-measured guardrail can still be biased on a poorly-measured primary. Check coverage for each metric separately.

Practical decision rule

Start here:

  • If assigned vs. measured distributions are close → stop, ship naive ITT.
  • If there's visible skew → compute reweighted ITT.
  • If reweighting creates spiky weights or slices are unstable → try MRP-like pooling + poststrat.
